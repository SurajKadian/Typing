The Code of Criminal Procedure: Arrest and Bail

Power of Arrest

The power to arrest a person is governed by the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973. An arrest may be made by a police officer with or without a warrant, depending upon the nature of the offence. Offences are classified as cognizable and non-cognizable. In a cognizable offence, the police have the power to arrest without a warrant, whereas in a non-cognizable offence, a warrant from a Magistrate is mandatory before any arrest is made.

Rights of the Arrested Person

Every person who is arrested has certain inviolable rights under the Constitution and the Code. Under Article 22 of the Constitution, no person shall be detained in custody without being informed of the grounds of arrest. The arrested person has the right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner of their choice.

No arrested person shall be detained beyond twenty-four hours without the authority of a Magistrate.

The Supreme Court in the landmark judgment of D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal laid down eleven binding guidelines to be followed by all law enforcement agencies at the time of arrest. These guidelines are mandatory and any deviation therefrom renders the concerned officer liable for contempt of court.

Bail

Bail is the conditional release of an arrested or accused person upon furnishing surety or security to appear before the court as and when required. Offences are divided into bailable and non-bailable categories. In a bailable offence, bail is a matter of right and the police officer or court must grant it. In a non-bailable offence, bail is a matter of discretion and the court may either grant or refuse it.

Anticipatory Bail

Section 438 of the Code provides for anticipatory bail. A person who has reason to believe that they may be arrested for a non-bailable offence may apply to the Sessions Court or the High Court for a direction that in the event of such arrest, they shall be released on bail. The court may impose such conditions as it thinks fit while granting anticipatory bail.

Remand

When an accused person is produced before a Magistrate and the investigation is not yet complete, the Magistrate may authorise the detention of the accused in police custody for a period not exceeding fifteen days in the whole. Thereafter, the accused may be remanded to judicial custody pending completion of the trial. The total period of detention before filing of the charge sheet shall not exceed sixty days or ninety days, depending upon the gravity of the offence.
