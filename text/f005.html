Law of Torts: Negligence and Liability

Definition of Tort

A tort is a civil wrong for which the remedy is a common law action for unliquidated damages. It is distinct from a crime, a breach of contract, and a breach of trust. The law of torts is primarily judge-made law and has evolved through centuries of judicial decisions in both England and India.

Negligence

Negligence is the most significant and frequently litigated tort in modern law. It consists of three essential elements. First, the defendant must owe a duty of care to the plaintiff. Second, there must be a breach of that duty. Third, the plaintiff must have suffered actual damage as a direct consequence of that breach.

The concept of duty of care was authoritatively established in the celebrated case of Donoghue v. Stevenson decided by the House of Lords in 1932. Lord Atkin propounded the neighbour principle, holding that one must take reasonable care to avoid acts or omissions which one can reasonably foresee would be likely to injure one's neighbour.

— Lord Atkin, Donoghue v. Stevenson, 1932

Standard of Care

The standard of care required is that of a reasonable man. This is an objective standard and does not take into account the personal characteristics of the defendant. A person who holds themselves out as possessing special skill or expertise is, however, judged by the standard of a reasonably competent professional in that field.

Res Ipsa Loquitur

The maxim res ipsa loquitur, meaning the thing speaks for itself, applies in cases where the mere occurrence of an accident raises a presumption of negligence against the defendant. Once this presumption arises, the burden of proof shifts to the defendant to rebut it by showing that the accident occurred without any fault on their part.

Strict Liability

The rule of strict liability was laid down in Rylands v. Fletcher in 1868. The rule states that a person who brings onto their land anything likely to do mischief if it escapes must keep it at their peril. If they fail to do so, they are prima facie answerable for all the natural consequences of its escape.

In India, the Supreme Court in M.C. Mehta v. Union of India evolved the doctrine of absolute liability, which goes further than strict liability by not recognising any of the exceptions available under the rule in Rylands v. Fletcher. Under absolute liability, an enterprise engaged in a hazardous activity is liable absolutely for any harm caused, regardless of fault or intention.
