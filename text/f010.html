The Hindu Succession Act, 1956

General Rules of Succession

The Hindu Succession Act, 1956 amended and codified the law relating to intestate succession among Hindus. The Act applies to any person who is a Hindu by religion in any of its forms or developments, including a Virashaiva, a Lingayat, or a follower of the Brahmo, Prarthana or Arya Samaj. It also applies to any person who is a Buddhist, Jain, or Sikh by religion.

Under the Act, the property of a Hindu dying intestate shall devolve according to the provisions of the Act. The devolution of property is governed by the rules relating to preferential heirs, the principle of simultaneous succession, and the doctrine that the nearer heir excludes the more remote. The Act abolished the distinction between obstructed and unobstructed heritage which existed under the old Hindu law.

Succession to Property of a Male Hindu

The property of a male Hindu dying intestate shall devolve firstly upon the heirs being the relatives specified in Class I of the Schedule. If there is no heir of Class I, then upon the heirs being the relatives specified in Class II of the Schedule. If there is no heir of any of the two classes, then upon the agnates of the deceased. If there is no agnate, then upon the cognates of the deceased.

Class I Heirs

The Class I heirs of a male Hindu include his son, daughter, widow, mother, son of a predeceased son, daughter of a predeceased son, son of a predeceased daughter, daughter of a predeceased daughter, widow of a predeceased son, son of a predeceased son of a predeceased son, daughter of a predeceased son of a predeceased son, and widow of a predeceased son of a predeceased son. Among the Class I heirs, the property is divided equally subject to certain special provisions.

Succession to Property of a Female Hindu

The property of a female Hindu dying intestate shall devolve according to the rules set out in Section 15 of the Act. The property devolves firstly upon the sons and daughters including the children of any predeceased son or daughter, and the husband. Secondly, upon the heirs of the husband. Thirdly, upon the mother and father. Fourthly, upon the heirs of the father. And lastly, upon the heirs of the mother.

Disqualifications

A person who commits murder or abets the commission of murder of an intestate or any other heir shall be disqualified from inheriting the property of that intestate. A convert from Hinduism to another religion and his descendants are not disqualified from inheriting the property of their Hindu relatives. However, a person who has ceased to be a Hindu by conversion to another religion cannot succeed to the property of a Hindu who dies intestate after such conversion.

Rights of Daughters

The Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005 made a landmark change by conferring upon daughters the same rights in coparcenary property as sons. The daughter of a coparcener shall by birth become a coparcener in her own right in the same manner as the son. She shall have the same rights in the coparcenary property as she would have had if she had been a son. The daughter shall be subject to the same liabilities in respect of the said coparcenary property as that of a son.

— As amended by the Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005

Marital Property

A widow of a predeceased son, a widow of a predeceased son of a predeceased son, and a widow of a brother shall not be entitled to succeed to the property of the intestate as a widow if she has remarried at the time the succession opens. Any heir who is related to an intestate as brother or sister does not inherit if the intestate is survived by any lineal descendant or by the father or mother of the intestate.
