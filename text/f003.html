Fundamental Rights under the Indian Constitution

Overview

Part III of the Constitution of India, spanning Articles 12 to 35, guarantees certain Fundamental Rights to every citizen. These rights are justiciable in nature, meaning any violation thereof can be challenged directly before the Supreme Court under Article 32 or before the High Court under Article 226.

Right to Equality

Articles 14 to 18 collectively constitute the Right to Equality. Article 14 guarantees equality before the law and equal protection of the laws to all persons within the territory of India. The doctrine of reasonable classification permits the state to treat different classes of persons differently, provided the classification is intelligible and has a rational nexus with the object sought to be achieved.

Article 15 prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. Article 16 ensures equality of opportunity in matters of public employment. Articles 17 and 18 abolish untouchability and titles respectively.

Right to Freedom

Article 19 guarantees six fundamental freedoms to citizens, including the freedom of speech and expression, freedom to assemble peaceably, freedom to form associations, freedom to move freely, freedom to reside and settle, and freedom to practise any profession.

These freedoms are not absolute and are subject to reasonable restrictions.

Articles 20, 21, and 22 protect the rights of accused persons and persons detained by the state. Article 21, which guarantees the right to life and personal liberty, has been interpreted expansively by the Supreme Court to include the right to livelihood, the right to health, the right to education, and the right to a clean environment.

Right against Exploitation

Articles 23 and 24 prohibit traffic in human beings, forced labour, and the employment of children below the age of fourteen years in any factory, mine, or other hazardous employment.

Right to Constitutional Remedies

Article 32 is regarded as the heart and soul of the Constitution. It empowers every citizen to move the Supreme Court for the enforcement of Fundamental Rights. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar described this article as the most important article of the Constitution, without which all other rights would be meaningless.

The Supreme Court may issue directions, orders, or writs including Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Certiorari, and Quo Warranto for the enforcement of any of the rights conferred by Part III of the Constitution.
