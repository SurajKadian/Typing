<h1>The Role of the Judiciary in a Democratic State</h1>

<h2>Introduction</h2>

<p>The <b>judiciary</b> is one of the three principal organs of the state. It functions alongside the <b>legislature</b> and the <b>executive</b> to ensure that the governance of a country remains just, fair, and accountable. In a <i>democratic</i> state, the independence of the judiciary is not merely a privilege but an <u>absolute necessity</u>. Without an independent judiciary, the rights of citizens cannot be effectively protected.</p>

<h2>Structure of the Judiciary</h2>

<p>The judicial system in most democratic countries is organised in a <i>hierarchical</i> manner. At the apex sits the <b>Supreme Court</b>, which holds the highest authority in all matters of law and constitutional interpretation. Below it are the <b>High Courts</b>, which exercise jurisdiction over individual states or provinces. At the lowest level, the <b>District Courts</b> and subordinate courts handle the majority of cases filed by ordinary citizens.</p>

<p>Each court has a defined <u>jurisdiction</u>, which refers to the legal authority of that court to hear and decide a particular category of case. Jurisdiction may be <i>original</i>, meaning the case is filed directly before that court, or <i>appellate</i>, meaning the court reviews the decision of a lower court.</p>

<h2>Functions of the Judiciary</h2>

<p>The primary function of the judiciary is the <b>settlement of disputes</b>. These disputes may arise between two private individuals, between an individual and the state, or between two organs of the government itself. In every such case, the court applies the relevant law and delivers a <u>binding judgment</u>.</p>

<p>A second important function is <b>judicial review</b>. Through this power, the superior courts examine whether any law passed by the legislature or any action taken by the executive is consistent with the provisions of the <i>Constitution</i>. If a law is found to be <u>unconstitutional</u>, the court has the authority to declare it void and unenforceable.</p>

<h3>Protection of Fundamental Rights</h3>

<p>The judiciary also serves as the <b>guardian of fundamental rights</b>. Citizens who believe their rights have been violated may approach the court for relief. The Supreme Court, in particular, has the power to issue <i>writs</i> such as <b>Habeas Corpus</b>, <b>Mandamus</b>, <b>Prohibition</b>, <b>Certiorari</b>, and <b>Quo Warranto</b> to enforce these rights and provide immediate remedy.</p>

<h2>Judicial Independence</h2>

<p style="text-align:center;"><b>An independent judiciary is the cornerstone of a free society.</b></p>

<p>To preserve its independence, judges are granted <u>security of tenure</u>. They cannot be removed from office arbitrarily or on the basis of their judgments. Their salaries and service conditions are charged to the <i>Consolidated Fund</i> so that they are not subject to annual parliamentary approval, which might otherwise be used as a means of <b>financial pressure</b>.</p>

<h2>Conclusion</h2>

<p>The judiciary, in its truest sense, is the <b>last resort</b> for the common citizen. It stands between the individual and the <i>arbitrary exercise of power</i> by the state. A strong, independent, and <u>impartial judiciary</u> is therefore not only a constitutional requirement but the very foundation upon which a just and democratic society must rest.</p>
