Law of Contract: Essential Elements

Definition and Scope

A contract is defined under Section 2(h) of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 as an agreement enforceable by law. Every contract is an agreement, but not every agreement is a contract. For an agreement to attain the status of a contract, it must satisfy certain essential conditions as prescribed by the Act.

Offer and Acceptance

The first essential element is a lawful offer made by one party and a lawful acceptance of that offer by the other. The offer must be communicated to the offeree, and the acceptance must be absolute and unconditional. A conditional acceptance amounts to a counter-offer and does not result in a binding contract.

The acceptance must be communicated in the prescribed manner. Where no manner is prescribed, it must be communicated in a reasonable and usual manner. Acceptance by conduct is also recognised under the Act, provided the conduct clearly indicates the intention to accept.

Consideration

Consideration is described as the price paid for a promise. It must be lawful, real, and not illusory. Under Section 2(d), consideration may move from the promisee or any other person. This distinguishes Indian contract law from English contract law, where consideration must always move from the promisee alone.

An agreement without consideration is void.

However, there are certain exceptions to this rule. A written and registered agreement made out of natural love and affection between parties standing in a near relation to each other is enforceable even without consideration.

Capacity to Contract

Every party entering into a contract must be competent to contract. Under Section 11, a person is competent if they have attained the age of majority, are of sound mind, and are not disqualified by any law to which they are subject.

A contract entered into by a minor is void ab initio — that is, void from the very beginning. The minor cannot ratify such a contract upon attaining majority. This position was firmly established in the landmark case of Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose decided by the Privy Council in 1903.

Free Consent

Consent is said to be free when it is not caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake. Where consent is obtained by any of these means, the contract becomes voidable at the option of the party whose consent was so obtained.

Lawful Object

The object of a contract must be lawful. Any agreement whose object is forbidden by law, fraudulent, or opposed to public policy shall be treated as void and shall have no legal effect whatsoever.
